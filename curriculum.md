# Master of Game Design and Development

36 credit hours, 12 courses, 4 semesters, 120 playable games

## Program Purpose

This master's degree is a hands-on studio program for designers, developers, artists, writers, producers, educators, researchers, and technologists preparing for advanced mid-career game roles. The program treats games as designed systems, software artifacts, cultural works, expressive media, and commercial products. Every course requires ten distinct playable games. Each game is small enough to finish, test, revise, and explain, but focused enough to demonstrate a specific professional competency.

Graduates leave with a public portfolio of 120 playable games: 90 core games and 30 track-specific games. Every game must be playable by someone other than the creator, include clear controls, contain a defined goal or interaction premise, and show the design principle named in the assignment.

The degree is engine-agnostic but engine-fluent. Students may work in Unity, Unreal, Godot, custom web stacks, Phaser, Three.js, Twine, Bitsy, Ren'Py, proprietary tools, or approved research platforms, but they must be able to explain why the chosen tool fits the assignment. Across the program, students are expected to encounter visual scripting, typed scripting, version control, data files, build pipelines, profiling, shaders or materials, procedural generation, AI-assisted workflows, local and networked play, accessibility standards, storefront preparation, and professional documentation.

The curriculum is built around ruthless completion. A small game that is finished, tested, revised, accessible, and explainable is valued above a larger idea that never becomes playable.

## Program Milestones and Review Gates

- End of Semester 1: Foundations Jury. Students present 30 games, identify their three strongest design competencies, and revise one game based on faculty critique.
- End of Semester 2: Professional Slice Review. Students present 60 games, a track direction statement, repository hygiene evidence, and one technically ambitious game with a documented production process.
- End of Semester 3: Specialization Review. Students present 90 games, a curated top-10 reel, a track thesis proposal, and evidence that their work fits a target professional role or creative field.
- End of Semester 4: Public Portfolio Defense. Students present 120 games, the track thesis collection, a top-12 curated portfolio, a resume-aligned competency map, and a public arcade or launcher.

Students may not pass a review gate with missing playable builds. Incomplete games can be revised, but the review conversation must be anchored in playable evidence.

## Elective Tracks

Students choose one track before GDV 513 and remain in that track through GDV 633.

### Storytelling and Digital Humanities

This track focuses on narrative systems, cultural archives, interactive history, adaptation, myth, memory, language, and public humanities. Students build games that communicate argument, context, interpretation, and point of view through play.

### Metamodern Interactive Art

This track focuses on expressive interaction, affect, sincerity and irony, ritual, installation thinking, poetic systems, performance, and experimental forms. Students build games that function as playable artworks and articulate a clear aesthetic position.

### Gaming for the AI Age

This track focuses on AI-native game design, procedural co-creation, intelligent agents, model-driven interfaces, synthetic content pipelines, evaluation, safety, and human-AI collaboration. Students build games that use AI as design material while preserving player agency and ethical accountability.

## Program-Wide Project Requirements

Unless an assignment specifies otherwise, every game must include:

- A playable build for web, desktop, or an approved target platform.
- A 30 to 90 second capture video or animated GIF showing the core interaction.
- A title screen or start state.
- A readable control reference inside the game or on the project page.
- A restart path without closing the application.
- A win, loss, completion, or reflective end state.
- At least 3 minutes of intentional play, even if the game is replayable in short rounds.
- Source files submitted through the course repository.
- A 250 to 500 word designer's note explaining the principle explored, what changed after testing, and what a future iteration would improve.
- A one-page postmortem identifying scope decisions, defects fixed, defects deferred, and the strongest evidence of learning.
- Evidence of at least two playtesters, including one observation-based change.
- Accessibility basics: remappable or documented controls, readable contrast, no required rapid flashing, captions or text alternatives for critical audio, pause or restart support, and no essential information communicated by color alone.
- Build instructions sufficient for a peer to run the game.
- Public-facing metadata: title, short description, genre tags, competency tags, credits, licenses, and known issues.

## Universal Master Grading Rubric

Every game is graded on a 100-point scale. Individual assignments specify the focus of the Objective Mastery category.

- Mechanics and technical execution: 30 points. A-level work runs reliably, has no game-breaking bugs, uses clean project organization, and demonstrates appropriate code, blueprint, script, data, shader, or tool architecture.
- Objective mastery: 30 points. A-level work isolates and explores the named weekly competency through play rather than through explanation alone.
- Game feel and player communication: 20 points. A-level work has responsive inputs, readable feedback, coherent audio/visual communication, and satisfying pacing appropriate to the assignment.
- Scope, polish, accessibility, and documentation: 20 points. A-level work is finished at the assigned scale, includes start/end/restart states, meets accessibility basics, includes required documentation, and shows playtest-informed revision.

Course-specific rubrics may redistribute emphasis inside these categories, but no assignment may ignore technical execution, objective mastery, player experience, or completion discipline.

## Accessibility and Responsibility Standard

Students use current game accessibility guidance as a recurring production reference, including control remapping, subtitle readability, color-independent information, difficulty options, sensory load controls, input timing alternatives, readable UI scale, and transparent onboarding. Projects involving analytics, AI, player-generated content, monetization, social play, cultural materials, or public participants must include an ethics note that identifies risks, mitigations, and residual concerns.

## Standard Grading Scale

- A-: 90-92

- B: 83-86
- B-: 80-82
- C+: 77-79

Late work may receive feedback but loses 10 percent per day unless an accommodation or production emergency agreement is approved in advance.

## Course 1: GDV 501 - Game Systems, Loops, and Player Experience

### Course Description

Students learn to design small but complete games around goals, rules, feedback, uncertainty, challenge, choice, and emotion. The course emphasizes analog-to-digital thinking, rapid iteration, player observation, and the ability to make a design legible through play instead of explanation.

### Course Outcomes

By the end of the course, students can define core loops, tune rule systems, design meaningful player choices, observe player behavior, and revise a game based on evidence.

### Projects

#### GDV501-P1: One-Button Timing Game

- Learning objectives: Design with extreme input constraint; create readable anticipation, action, and result states; tune difficulty through timing windows.
- Execution details: Build a game controlled by one button only. The player must learn a timing rule, fail safely, and improve within three attempts. Include three difficulty tiers and visual/audio feedback for early, perfect, and late input.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts timing design and fairness; 20 pts clarity of feedback; 20 pts complete playable states; 15 pts tuning evidence from playtests; 15 pts designer's note and accessibility.

#### GDV501-P2: Risk-Reward Collection Game

- Learning objectives: Model risk, reward, and opportunity cost; communicate danger without text dependency; balance short-term gain against long-term survival.
- Execution details: Build a top-down or side-view collection game where every valuable pickup increases danger, changes movement, or alters the map. Include at least two viable strategies and a score summary at the end.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts meaningful risk-reward structure; 20 pts balance across strategies; 20 pts responsive core loop; 15 pts end-state scoring and feedback; 15 pts documentation and playtest revision.

#### GDV501-P3: Resource Conversion Puzzle

- Learning objectives: Design resources as verbs; create puzzle constraints from conversion rules; teach through level sequence.
- Execution details: Build a puzzle game with at least three resource types and two conversion actions. Include five authored levels that introduce, combine, and test the rules without a tutorial paragraph.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts resource economy clarity; 25 pts puzzle progression; 15 pts interface readability; 15 pts bug-free completion path; 15 pts reflection and test notes.

#### GDV501-P4: Hidden Information Bluffing Game

- Learning objectives: Use uncertainty as a system; create readable tells and counterplay; design AI or local multiplayer decision moments.
- Execution details: Build a two-sided game against another player or a simple bot where private information changes the value of choices. Include at least five rounds per match and a match recap showing key reveals.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts hidden-information design; 20 pts counterplay and strategic depth; 20 pts functional opponent or local multiplayer flow; 15 pts match recap clarity; 15 pts documentation.

#### GDV501-P5: Movement Verb Game

- Learning objectives: Make movement itself the primary mechanic; shape challenge through spatial affordances; tune acceleration, friction, jump, dash, glide, or equivalent properties.
- Execution details: Build a game with one distinctive movement verb and five short challenge rooms. The game must be completable without combat, inventory, or score as the main attraction.
- Grading rubric: 35 pts movement feel and consistency; 20 pts level use of the verb; 15 pts readable hazards and goals; 15 pts completion polish; 15 pts iteration evidence.

#### GDV501-P6: Choice Consequence Microgame

- Learning objectives: Represent consequence in mechanics; distinguish cosmetic choice from systemic choice; build replayable branches.
- Execution details: Build a 5 to 8 minute game where at least three player decisions change later rules, available actions, or ending conditions. Avoid simple dialogue-only branching unless choices affect play.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts systemic consequences; 20 pts replayable structure; 20 pts clarity of changed state; 15 pts narrative or thematic coherence; 15 pts notes and playtest evidence.

#### GDV501-P7: Cooperative Constraint Game

- Learning objectives: Design cooperation through asymmetric limits; create communication pressure; evaluate whether cooperation is required or optional.
- Execution details: Build a two-player local game or single-player game controlling two entities. Each role must have a unique limitation and a unique power. Include at least four encounters that require coordination.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts cooperative dependency; 20 pts role asymmetry; 20 pts encounter progression; 15 pts input and camera usability; 15 pts documentation.

#### GDV501-P8: Dynamic Difficulty Game

- Learning objectives: Adapt challenge to player performance; measure player state without being intrusive; preserve dignity and agency when assistance is provided.
- Execution details: Build a game that tracks at least two performance signals and modifies challenge during play. Include a debug overlay or post-game report showing what adapted and why.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts adaptive design logic; 20 pts fairness and transparency; 20 pts implementation reliability; 15 pts player report/debug explanation; 15 pts reflection.

#### GDV501-P9: Emergent Toy to Game Conversion

- Learning objectives: Differentiate toy, puzzle, and game; discover affordances through experimentation; add goals without killing expressive play.
- Execution details: First build a toy-like interaction system, then add goals, constraints, and feedback to turn it into a game. Submit the final game and a short captured comparison of the toy state and game state.
- Grading rubric: 25 pts expressive interaction; 25 pts successful goal/rule conversion; 20 pts player freedom retained; 15 pts presentation of before/after; 15 pts notes.

#### GDV501-P10: Ten-Minute Festival Game

- Learning objectives: Scope a complete experience for public play; create immediate onboarding; design for spectators and fast turnover.
- Execution details: Build a polished game intended for a classroom festival station. A new player must understand the goal in 30 seconds and finish or restart within 10 minutes. Include a public-facing project page.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts onboarding and accessibility; 25 pts complete festival-ready play; 20 pts system clarity and engagement; 15 pts polish; 10 pts public page and reflection.

## Course 2: GDV 502 - Gameplay Programming and Rapid Tools

### Course Description

Students develop the technical fluency needed to implement prototypes quickly, debug behavior, structure code, and build small tools that accelerate design iteration. Assignments may be completed in Unity, Godot, Unreal, Phaser, custom web stacks, or an approved equivalent.

### Course Outcomes

Students can implement gameplay loops, state machines, collision, data-driven content, save systems, debugging interfaces, and designer-facing tools.

### Projects

#### GDV502-P1: State Machine Arcade Game

- Learning objectives: Implement explicit game states; separate input, update, and rendering concerns; handle transitions reliably.
- Execution details: Build an arcade game with menu, countdown, play, pause, game over, and results states. State transitions must be visible in code and testable through a debug key or menu.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts state architecture; 20 pts gameplay completeness; 20 pts transition reliability; 15 pts code readability; 15 pts debug/test documentation.

#### GDV502-P2: Collision and Physics Challenge

- Learning objectives: Use collision layers and triggers; distinguish physics simulation from authored movement; debug spatial interactions.
- Execution details: Build a game where the main challenge depends on collision, bounce, sliding, pushing, or gravity. Include a debug visualization toggle for collision bounds, velocity, or contact state.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts correct collision behavior; 20 pts physics-based level design; 20 pts debug visualization; 15 pts feel and tuning; 15 pts code notes.

#### GDV502-P3: Data-Driven Wave Game

- Learning objectives: Store gameplay parameters outside core logic; build content from structured data; enable rapid balancing.
- Execution details: Build a survival, rhythm, tower defense, or encounter game where waves, enemies, items, or events are loaded from JSON, CSV, scriptable objects, resources, or equivalent data assets. Include at least six authored waves.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts data-driven architecture; 20 pts content variety; 20 pts balancing and pacing; 15 pts designer usability; 15 pts documentation.

#### GDV502-P4: Save, Load, and Persistence Game

- Learning objectives: Implement persistence safely; decide what game state matters; communicate saved progress to players.
- Execution details: Build a game with progress that survives restart. Save at least player position/progression, unlocked content, settings, and score/history. Include reset and corrupt-save fallback behavior.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts persistence correctness; 20 pts player-facing save clarity; 20 pts reset/fallback handling; 15 pts testing evidence; 15 pts code organization.

#### GDV502-P5: Tool-Assisted Level Builder

- Learning objectives: Build internal tools; represent levels as editable data; separate authoring from runtime play.
- Execution details: Build a small game plus a level editing tool, in-engine editor workflow, or external data workflow. Submit three levels made with the tool and a one-page tool guide for another designer.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts tool usefulness; 20 pts runtime integration; 20 pts quality of authored levels; 15 pts usability of guide; 15 pts maintainability.

#### GDV502-P6: Procedural Spawner Game

- Learning objectives: Use randomization responsibly; constrain probability; support replayability without unfairness.
- Execution details: Build a game that generates enemies, pickups, rooms, or challenges at runtime. Include seed support or a replay code so the same generated session can be reproduced.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts procedural logic; 20 pts fairness constraints; 20 pts replayability; 15 pts seed/reproduction support; 15 pts notes.

#### GDV502-P7: Input Remapping Game

- Learning objectives: Support multiple input devices; abstract actions from hardware; improve accessibility through control options.
- Execution details: Build a game playable with keyboard and at least one additional input mode such as gamepad, mouse-only, touch, or adaptive simplified controls. Include an input settings screen or clearly documented remapping path.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts input abstraction; 20 pts device support; 20 pts settings usability; 15 pts accessible design choices; 15 pts test report.

#### GDV502-P8: Behavior Tree or Utility AI Game

- Learning objectives: Implement structured non-player behavior; debug decision logic; create enemies or companions that signal intent.
- Execution details: Build a game with at least two AI actors using a behavior tree, utility scoring, finite-state machine with clear hierarchy, or equivalent architecture. Include a debug display of current decisions.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts AI architecture; 20 pts gameplay use of AI; 20 pts readable telegraphing; 15 pts debug display; 15 pts code commentary.

#### GDV502-P9: Performance Budget Game

- Learning objectives: Profile runtime performance; identify bottlenecks; optimize without removing core design value.
- Execution details: Build a game with many objects, effects, or simulation elements, then profile and optimize it to meet an agreed frame-rate budget. Submit before/after measurements.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts profiling method; 20 pts successful optimization; 20 pts preserved gameplay; 15 pts measurement clarity; 15 pts code quality.

#### GDV502-P10: Modular Prototype Kit Game

- Learning objectives: Create reusable gameplay modules; document APIs for future self and teammates; assemble a complete game from components.
- Execution details: Build a small game using at least five reusable components such as health, inventory, interactable, timer, spawner, dialogue trigger, or ability module. Include a sample scene demonstrating each component.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts modularity; 20 pts complete final game; 20 pts component demo scene; 15 pts documentation; 15 pts maintainable code.

## Course 3: GDV 503 - Game Feel, UI, Audio, and Visual Communication

### Course Description

Students study moment-to-moment feedback, visual hierarchy, interface design, sound, animation, juice, accessibility, and production-ready presentation. The course emphasizes how games communicate state through sensory design.

### Course Outcomes

Students can design satisfying feedback, readable interfaces, cohesive audiovisual style, and player-facing options that make small games feel complete.

### Projects

#### GDV503-P1: Impact and Hit-Stop Game

- Learning objectives: Use animation timing to communicate force; apply hit-stop, screen shake, particles, and sound without obscuring play; tune feedback intensity.
- Execution details: Build an action game centered on impacts. Include at least three feedback layers per successful action and an options toggle to reduce camera shake or intense effects.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts impact readability; 20 pts feedback layering; 20 pts playability under effects; 15 pts accessibility toggle; 15 pts reflection.

#### GDV503-P2: Diegetic Interface Game

- Learning objectives: Integrate UI into the game world; reduce HUD dependency; communicate health, score, or objectives through objects and environment.
- Execution details: Build a game where at least two essential status indicators are diegetic. Players should not need a conventional corner HUD to understand progress or danger.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts diegetic UI clarity; 20 pts world integration; 20 pts gameplay completeness; 15 pts visual hierarchy; 15 pts playtest evidence.

#### GDV503-P3: Audio-First Game

- Learning objectives: Design gameplay around listening; mix audio cues by priority; provide non-audio alternatives for accessibility.
- Execution details: Build a game where sound is central to navigation, timing, stealth, rhythm, or discovery. Include captions, visual pulses, haptics, or other alternatives for critical cues.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts audio mechanic design; 20 pts mix clarity; 20 pts accessible alternatives; 15 pts complete game loop; 15 pts documentation.

#### GDV503-P4: Color and Shape Language Game

- Learning objectives: Use color, silhouette, and shape to distinguish roles; design for color vision accessibility; reinforce rules visually.
- Execution details: Build a game with at least five object types that are understandable by shape and behavior, not color alone. Include a colorblind-safe palette or alternate mode.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts visual language consistency; 20 pts accessibility; 20 pts rule readability; 15 pts aesthetic cohesion; 15 pts notes.

#### GDV503-P5: Menu as Game

- Learning objectives: Treat menu navigation as interaction design; build settings and progression screens; reduce friction before play.
- Execution details: Build a game where the front end, options, level select, or inventory screen includes meaningful interaction. Include keyboard/gamepad or equivalent non-pointer navigation.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts interface usability; 20 pts menu-game integration; 20 pts options completeness; 15 pts input support; 15 pts documentation.

#### GDV503-P6: Animation-Driven Character Game

- Learning objectives: Use animation states to communicate intention; connect animation timing to mechanics; avoid animation that lies about collision or control.
- Execution details: Build a game with a character or avatar that has at least eight readable animation states or pose changes tied to mechanics. Include at least one cancel, recovery, or anticipation rule.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts animation readability; 20 pts mechanic-animation alignment; 20 pts control responsiveness; 15 pts polish; 15 pts iteration notes.

#### GDV503-P7: Minimalist Readability Game

- Learning objectives: Communicate with limited assets; establish hierarchy through spacing, motion, contrast, and rhythm; remove nonessential information.
- Execution details: Build a complete game using no more than three colors, three sound families, and simple geometric forms. The constraint should improve readability rather than feel unfinished.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts clarity under constraint; 20 pts cohesive minimalist style; 20 pts complete design; 15 pts player comprehension; 15 pts notes.

#### GDV503-P8: Emotional Interface Game

- Learning objectives: Connect UI state to emotion; use typography, pacing, sound, and layout to shape feeling; maintain usability under expressive presentation.
- Execution details: Build a game where the interface changes in response to player condition, story state, or system pressure. The UI must remain readable and controllable at all times.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts expressive UI concept; 20 pts usability; 20 pts technical implementation; 15 pts emotional coherence; 15 pts reflection.

#### GDV503-P9: Accessibility Retrofit Game

- Learning objectives: Audit barriers; design options for diverse players; evaluate whether an accessibility feature changes the core challenge appropriately.
- Execution details: Start with one previous game or a provided prototype and retrofit at least six accessibility improvements across controls, visuals, audio, pace, text, and difficulty. Submit before/after builds or recordings.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts quality of accessibility improvements; 20 pts preservation of core design; 20 pts implementation completeness; 15 pts before/after evidence; 15 pts audit rationale.

#### GDV503-P10: Polished Micro-Arcade Cabinet

- Learning objectives: Combine game feel, UI, audio, and presentation; design for public play; present a coherent brand identity.
- Execution details: Build a polished arcade-style game with title treatment, attract loop, tutorial prompt, sound mix, score feedback, and end screen. It must be understandable from a standing demo station.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts complete audiovisual polish; 20 pts onboarding; 20 pts game feel; 15 pts public-play readiness; 15 pts documentation.

## Course 4: GDV 511 - Level Design, Spaces, and Encounter Craft

### Course Description

Students design space as a gameplay system. They study metrics, affordances, pathing, pacing, encounter composition, landmarks, environmental storytelling, navigation, and level documentation.

### Course Outcomes

Students can block out, test, revise, and present playable levels that teach mechanics, stage encounters, and guide players without overreliance on text.

### Projects

#### GDV511-P1: Metrics Gym

- Learning objectives: Establish player metrics; build spaces that fit movement; test jump, sightline, cover, and interaction distances.
- Execution details: Build a playable metrics gym for one avatar controller. Include labeled test zones for movement, camera, interaction, hazard spacing, and traversal timing, then include a short challenge using those metrics.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts metric accuracy; 20 pts test coverage; 20 pts playable challenge; 15 pts labeling and documentation; 15 pts revision evidence.

#### GDV511-P2: Tutorial Without Text

- Learning objectives: Teach through spatial arrangement; introduce one concept at a time; verify comprehension through player action.
- Execution details: Build a level that teaches a mechanic without tutorial text, voiceover, or pop-up instructions. Use gating, repetition, framing, and consequence to teach.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts nonverbal teaching; 20 pts pacing; 20 pts completion clarity; 15 pts playtest observation; 15 pts polish.

#### GDV511-P3: Hub and Spoke Exploration Game

- Learning objectives: Design navigable hubs; create landmarks and return paths; reward optional exploration.
- Execution details: Build a small hub with at least four spokes. Each spoke must contain a distinct challenge and a reward or information piece that changes how the hub is perceived or used.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts hub navigation; 20 pts spoke variety; 20 pts reward design; 15 pts landmark readability; 15 pts documentation.

#### GDV511-P4: Stealth Encounter Level

- Learning objectives: Design patrol routes, vision cones, cover, and escape paths; balance failure states; create readable stealth affordances.
- Execution details: Build a stealth game or level with at least three guards or sensors, two routes to the objective, and one recoverable mistake state before failure.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts stealth encounter design; 20 pts route choice; 20 pts feedback and readability; 15 pts recoverability; 15 pts notes.

#### GDV511-P5: Combat Arena with Phases

- Learning objectives: Shape combat through space; use enemy composition and arena changes; pace intensity across phases.
- Execution details: Build an arena encounter with at least three phases. Each phase must alter space, enemy behavior, objective pressure, or resource availability.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts encounter pacing; 20 pts arena affordances; 20 pts phase differentiation; 15 pts fairness and telegraphing; 15 pts testing.

#### GDV511-P6: Environmental Story Game

- Learning objectives: Communicate narrative through arrangement; use props and spatial sequence; avoid exposition as the main delivery method.
- Execution details: Build an explorable space that tells a story through environment, objects, locked/unlocked paths, and player discovery. Text is allowed but cannot carry more than half of the meaning.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts environmental storytelling; 20 pts spatial sequencing; 20 pts interaction design; 15 pts interpretive clarity; 15 pts reflection.

#### GDV511-P7: Verticality and Sightline Game

- Learning objectives: Use height as design material; control visibility and anticipation; support navigation across layered spaces.
- Execution details: Build a level where vertical movement or layered sightlines matter to strategy. Include at least three moments where seeing a destination, threat, or reward changes player choice.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts vertical design; 20 pts sightline composition; 20 pts navigation clarity; 15 pts mechanic integration; 15 pts notes.

#### GDV511-P8: Constraint Remix Level

- Learning objectives: Reuse a mechanic across multiple contexts; create variety through constraints; stage difficulty progression.
- Execution details: Build a game with one core mechanic and five compact levels. Each level must introduce a new constraint such as time, enemy, resource, space, information, or control limitation.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts constraint design; 20 pts level variety; 20 pts progression; 15 pts clarity; 15 pts playtest evidence.

#### GDV511-P9: Nonlinear Mission Game

- Learning objectives: Design objectives that can be completed in multiple orders; manage dependency and player planning; support meaningful route choice.
- Execution details: Build a mission with at least three objectives available in nonlinear order. At least one objective must change the state or difficulty of another.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts nonlinear structure; 20 pts dependency design; 20 pts route choice; 15 pts state communication; 15 pts documentation.

#### GDV511-P10: Level Design Portfolio Piece

- Learning objectives: Present level design professionally; document intent and iteration; produce a shippable small level.
- Execution details: Build a polished level for an existing prototype or your own controller. Submit the playable build, annotated map, beat chart, and one-page postmortem.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts level quality; 20 pts professional documentation; 20 pts polish and playability; 15 pts iteration evidence; 15 pts portfolio presentation.

## Course 5: GDV 512 - Applied Game Mathematics, Technical Art, and Production Pipelines

### Course Description

Students learn to turn mathematical and technical-art concepts into playable systems while maintaining professional production habits. The course covers vectors, trigonometry, physics, shaders/materials, procedural meshes, optimization, version control, build review, bug tracking, and milestone delivery.

### Course Outcomes

Students can implement math-driven mechanics, create readable shader and material effects, profile technical systems, scope production, review builds, communicate risk, and deliver stable technical slices.

### Projects

#### GDV512-P1: 48-Hour Scope Game

- Learning objectives: Scope aggressively; prioritize a playable core; separate must-have from nice-to-have.
- Execution details: Build a complete game in 48 hours using a written feature lock after hour 8. Submit the feature lock, final build, and a list of cut features with reasons.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts scope discipline; 20 pts playable completion; 20 pts feature prioritization; 15 pts build stability; 15 pts retrospective.

#### GDV512-P2: Vector Billiards Game

- Learning objectives: Apply vector reflection, dot products, collision normals, and angle prediction; expose mathematical behavior through readable play.
- Execution details: Build a billiards, ricochet shooter, laser puzzle, or pinball-like game where bounce angles are central. Include an optional debug overlay showing incoming vector, surface normal, and reflected vector.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts mathematical correctness; 20 pts readable physics feedback; 20 pts complete game loop; 15 pts debug visualization; 15 pts tuning and documentation.

#### GDV512-P3: Trigonometric Motion Game

- Learning objectives: Use sine, cosine, phase, amplitude, frequency, and interpolation to design predictable motion; synchronize moving systems.
- Execution details: Build a platformer, rhythm dodger, boss pattern, racing obstacle course, or puzzle game where at least five objects move through authored trigonometric functions. Include one section where changing phase relationships changes the challenge.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts trig implementation; 20 pts motion readability; 20 pts level or encounter design; 15 pts synchronization/tuning; 15 pts notes.

#### GDV512-P4: Planetary Gravity Game

- Learning objectives: Implement force, mass, velocity, acceleration, and distance-based attraction; stabilize simulation for playability.
- Execution details: Build a spaceship, orbit, slingshot, or gravity-puzzle game where celestial bodies attract or repel the player. Include at least three bodies with different masses and a trajectory preview or training mode.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts physics model; 20 pts simulation stability; 20 pts player-facing readability; 15 pts trajectory/help tools; 15 pts reflection.

#### GDV512-P5: Screen-Space Outline or Highlight Shader Game

- Learning objectives: Use materials, render passes, depth, normals, masks, or engine-equivalent techniques to communicate affordances; separate visual effect from game logic.
- Execution details: Build a game where interactable, dangerous, hidden, or selected objects are communicated through a custom outline, highlight, x-ray, silhouette, or screen-space effect. Include at least three gameplay states with distinct visual treatment.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts shader/material implementation; 20 pts gameplay readability; 20 pts visual polish; 15 pts performance and artifact control; 15 pts technical breakdown.

- Grading rubric: 30 pts systemic depth; 20 pts rule elegance; 20 pts player discovery; 15 pts clarity; 15 pts documentation.

#### GDV632-P5: Technical Showpiece Game

- Execution details: Build a game featuring a technical focus such as shaders, procedural animation, AI, networking, physics, tools, performance, data visualization, or unusual input. Include a technical breakdown.

#### GDV632-P6: Art Direction Showpiece Game

#### GDV632-P7: Emotional Arc Game

- Learning objectives: Shape emotion across beginning, middle, and end; use mechanics and pacing rather than exposition alone; test audience response.
- Execution details: Build a 5 to 10 minute game with a deliberate emotional arc. Include a beat map showing how mechanics, audio, visuals, and stakes change over time.

## Course 12: GDV 633 - Track Studio III: Thesis Microgames

### Course Description

Students complete the track sequence by building ten thesis microgames. Together, the ten games form a playable argument about the student's professional direction and become the centerpiece of the graduate portfolio.

### Course Outcomes

Students can define a thesis through multiple playable works, produce public-facing documentation, respond to critique, and position their practice within a professional field.

### Projects

#### GDV633-P1: Thesis Question Game

- Learning objectives: Convert a thesis question into play; establish stakes; invite investigation rather than merely state a topic.
- Execution details: Build a game that introduces the central question for the track thesis collection. Storytelling and Digital Humanities projects should identify the interpretive question. Metamodern Interactive Art projects should identify the aesthetic or experiential tension. Gaming for the AI Age projects should identify the AI design problem, capability, or risk.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts thesis-question clarity; 20 pts playable investigation; 20 pts track fit; 15 pts polish; 15 pts statement.

#### GDV633-P2: Method Demonstration Game

- Learning objectives: Demonstrate the student's primary method; make process visible through play; establish repeatable craft.
- Execution details: Build a game that foregrounds one method such as close reading, procedural poetics, archival interpretation, participatory performance, model prompting, agent design, or dataset critique. Include a method note.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts method clarity; 20 pts execution quality; 20 pts track sophistication; 15 pts method note; 15 pts reflection.

#### GDV633-P3: Audience Challenge Game

- Learning objectives: Confront audience assumptions; design respectful friction; measure interpretation.
- Execution details: Build a game that asks the intended audience to reconsider a habit, belief, expectation, genre convention, or AI interaction pattern. Conduct at least three targeted playtests.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts audience challenge design; 20 pts respectful friction; 20 pts playtest insight; 15 pts game completeness; 15 pts notes.

#### GDV633-P4: Material Experiment Game

- Learning objectives: Treat media, interface, model, archive, installation context, or hardware as material; discover affordances; document limitations.
- Execution details: Build a game using an unusual material constraint such as scanned documents, physical controllers, live performance, generated assets, speech input, public-domain media, model outputs, or found data.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts material experiment; 20 pts integration with play; 20 pts technical/artistic control; 15 pts limitation documentation; 15 pts reflection.

#### GDV633-P5: Comparative Pair Game

- Learning objectives: Show how a design changes under two interpretations; compare mechanics; make contrast playable.
- Execution details: Build one game with two modes, two versions, or two playable stances. The difference must represent a meaningful theoretical, aesthetic, cultural, or AI-system comparison.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts comparative design; 20 pts clarity of contrast; 20 pts playability of both versions; 15 pts analysis; 15 pts polish.

#### GDV633-P6: Public Context Game

- Learning objectives: Design for a specific venue or community; account for facilitation, access, and presentation; prepare public documentation.
- Execution details: Build a game for a concrete public context such as classroom, museum, gallery, conference, online festival, workplace training, creator community, or AI literacy workshop. Include installation or facilitation notes.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts context fit; 20 pts facilitation readiness; 20 pts accessibility; 15 pts documentation; 15 pts playtest evidence.

#### GDV633-P7: Critique Response Game

- Learning objectives: Use critique as production input; identify which feedback serves the thesis; revise without losing authorship.
- Execution details: Present a prior thesis microgame for critique, then build a new game or major revision that directly responds to three critique points. Include a response matrix.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts critique integration; 20 pts design improvement; 20 pts thesis continuity; 15 pts response matrix; 15 pts reflection.

#### GDV633-P8: Professional Positioning Game

- Learning objectives: Build for a target professional role; demonstrate marketable skill; connect track identity to career direction.
- Execution details: Build a game aimed at a specific role such as narrative designer, technical designer, gameplay programmer, tools developer, UX designer, producer, AI game designer, creative technologist, educator, or interactive artist. Include a role-alignment note.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts role relevance; 20 pts skill demonstration; 20 pts portfolio readiness; 15 pts role-alignment note; 15 pts polish.

#### GDV633-P9: Thesis Synthesis Game

- Learning objectives: Combine findings from the thesis collection; create a culminating playable statement; show advanced craft.
- Execution details: Build the strongest standalone thesis microgame. It must synthesize at least four prior thesis concerns and be prepared for external review.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts synthesis; 20 pts final play quality; 20 pts track mastery; 15 pts external-review readiness; 15 pts documentation.

#### GDV633-P10: Thesis Collection Launcher

- Learning objectives: Curate ten thesis microgames as one portfolio work; guide audience interpretation; prepare for professional presentation.
- Execution details: Build a playable launcher, website, exhibition menu, or anthology wrapper containing all ten thesis microgames or links to them. Include project statements, credits, accessibility notes, and a 1,500 to 2,000 word thesis reflection.
- Grading rubric: 30 pts collection curation; 20 pts usability and access; 20 pts quality of thesis framing; 15 pts completeness of documentation; 15 pts final presentation.

## Track-Specific Studio Progressions

The Track Studio assignments above define the formal project requirements. The following progressions define how each track should interpret those requirements so that the student's 30 track games form a coherent specialization rather than a miscellaneous set of experiments.

### Storytelling and Digital Humanities Progression

GDV 513 emphasizes narrative mechanics and textual play. Students should build games involving branching parables, dialogue duels, epistolary mystery, memory palaces, procedural poetry, unreliable narration, myth retellings, rule-changing text, and choice-with-consequence vignettes. Each game must identify the interpretive problem it explores and distinguish playable interpretation from static exposition.

GDV 623 emphasizes archives, adaptation, and procedural humanities. Students should build games involving archive exploration, public-domain adaptation, manuscript variants, historical maps, corpus remix, translation choices, annotation mechanics, oral-history reconstruction, museum objects, and critical editions as playable spaces. Each game must include source context, citation, and a note about what the game necessarily leaves out or transforms.

GDV 633 emphasizes interactive nonfiction, pedagogy, cultural memory, and literary anthology work. Students should build games involving documentary choice, civic systems, debate mechanics, moral dilemma engines, timeline reconstruction, witness/testimony structures, educational puzzle sequences, rhetoric-as-combat, cultural-memory spaces, playable essays, and final authorial literary games. The thesis collection must make a defensible humanities argument through interaction.

Required track artifacts: citation notes, source-transformation notes, sensitivity or cultural-context notes when relevant, and an interpretive statement explaining how mechanics carry meaning.

### Metamodern Interactive Art Progression

GDV 513 emphasizes play, sincerity, oscillation, and affect. Students should build games involving irony/sincerity switching, hope after failure, absurd ritual, cozy dread, sacred/secular objects, earnest embarrassment, player confession, repetition with difference, wonder mechanics, and both/and oscillation. Each game must name the affective tension it stages.

GDV 623 emphasizes embodied, performative, surreal, and installation-oriented work. Students should build games involving voice control, webcam or camera interaction, audience voting, two-person trust, posture or breath, sound-reactive systems, physical-object controllers, gallery loops, performance scores, silence as mechanic, dream logic, glitch transcendence, minimalist awe, and anti-addictive contemplative pacing. Each game must include a facilitation or installation note when public context matters.

GDV 633 emphasizes festival-ready interactive art portfolios. Students should build games involving one-minute art pieces, ten-minute installation pieces, public kiosk works, mobile contemplative works, projection-ready works, audience-coauthored works, live performance games, museum education works, personal myth works, and a signature festival submission. The thesis collection must be presentable as a coherent exhibition, performance set, or online art arcade.

Required track artifacts: artist statements, installation/facilitation notes, audience consent notes when participation is captured or exposed, documentation images/video, and a statement of aesthetic lineage.

### Gaming for the AI Age Progression

GDV 513 emphasizes AI-augmented prototyping and content pipelines. Students should build games involving AI-assisted concept-to-prototype workflows, prompt-generated sprites or textures, AI-assisted dialogue, synthetic soundscapes, generated quests, human-edited AI levels, asset provenance, prompt-injection-resistant NPC toys, AI-assisted localization, and a comparison between a game made with and without AI assistance. Each game must distinguish generated material from human-authored integration.

GDV 623 emphasizes NPCs, agents, player modeling, and generated worlds. Students should build games involving behavior-tree NPCs, utility-AI creatures, GOAP planning, LLM conversation NPCs, memory-bearing companions, emotion-state NPCs, adaptive difficulty, player-style classifiers, multi-agent settlement simulations, NPC failure-mode museums, procedural dungeons, grammar-generated stories, cellular automata worlds, designer-facing generation tools, and generated-world QA testbeds.

GDV 633 emphasizes AI-native studio practice and governance. Students should build games involving human-in-the-loop AI play, AI dungeon-master prototypes, personalized narrative, AI moderation, local-model toys, retrieval-augmented lore, AI labor transparency, anti-bias NPC audits, model failure as gameplay, and a final AI-native vertical slice. The thesis collection must demonstrate not only capability, but also containment, evaluation, disclosure, and player agency.

Required track artifacts: AI provenance logs, prompt or system-card summaries when safe to disclose, model or dataset notes, failure-mode tests, latency/fallback plans, safety mitigations, and a statement explaining which decisions remain under human control.

## Portfolio Expectations at Graduation

Each graduate's final portfolio must include:

- 120 playable games.
- A curated "top 12" collection representing the student's professional identity.
- At least one polished game from each semester.
- A track thesis collection of 10 games.
- Source repositories or equivalent technical archives for review.
- Public pages with controls, credits, accessibility notes, and design statements.
- A 2 to 4 minute reel showing gameplay from at least 20 projects.
- At least five 30 to 90 second single-game clips suitable for recruiters, festivals, grant panels, or portfolio reviewers.
- A press-kit page for the top project, including capsule/key art, logo or title treatment, screenshots, short description, long description, credits, and contact information.
- A technical breakdown for at least three projects, including one systems/code project, one production/tooling project, and one track-specific project.
- A written portfolio map connecting projects to competencies, tools, and target roles.

## Competency Map

The 120-game portfolio demonstrates:

- Core design: loops, rules, pacing, choice, uncertainty, economy, feedback.
- Technical design: programming patterns, tools, data, AI, networking, optimization.
- Production: scoping, version control, QA, milestones, collaboration, publishing.
- Presentation: UI, audio, animation, accessibility, art direction, portfolio writing.
- Research and ethics: player research, data responsibility, inclusive design, cultural context.
- Track specialization: digital humanities authorship, interactive art practice, or AI-age game development.

## Assessment Philosophy

The program rewards finished playable work, clear thinking, responsible iteration, and professional presentation. Ambition is valued when it is matched by execution. Students are expected to make many small games because repeated completion teaches scope, taste, resilience, and craft more reliably than a single oversized project.
